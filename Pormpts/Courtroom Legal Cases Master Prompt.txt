→ Short sentences: 4-8 words for the most dramatic revelations
→ Start with facts — then layer in the emotional weight
→ Conversational but authoritative — like a legal analyst explaining
   a case to an interested friend

PHASE 3 — HOOK OPENING LINE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The hook introduces the case and creates immediate curiosity
about the legal or emotional conflict.

HOOK RULES:
→ Maximum 14 words
→ Introduces who is suing/accusing/asking whom and why
→ Hints at the surprise or emotional conflict
→ Never reveals the outcome

COURTROOM HOOK FORMULAS:

"A [age]-year-old [role] dragged [their] own [family role]
 into court — asking for something nobody expected."

"[Role] vs. [role]. The question before the judge —
 who was really in the wrong?"

"Most [role] would never do this. But this [role]
 felt [they] had no other choice."

"We've all heard of [common situation]. But this case
 took it to a courtroom."

"She brought her own [family member] to court.
 The reason? Something most of us would never consider."

"Behind every courtroom case is a human story.
 This one starts with a [age]-year-old [role]."

"They were supposed to be family. Instead —
 they ended up on opposite sides of a courtroom."

PHASE 4 — NARRATION SCRIPT
════════════════════════════════════════════════════

CRITICAL WORD COUNT RULE:
Your script must contain the EXACT same number
of words as the original courtroom dialogue.
Not approximate — EXACT.

SPEAKING PACE RULE:
Write for 150–170 WPM delivery.
This allows room for natural pauses after dramatic
revelations and gives weight to the most important lines.

SYNC RULE:
Each narration line must match what is happening
in the courtroom at that exact moment. The narrator
explains the legal context and emotional subtext
BEHIND what is being said.

WHAT THIS STYLE REQUIRES:
→ Present the facts of the case clearly
→ Explain WHY someone is saying what they're saying
  (their motivation, their pain, their fear)
→ Translate legal language for a general audience
→ Highlight the emotional stakes underneath formal proceedings
→ Point out body language, tone of voice, hesitation
→ Let the most dramatic moments land with space and silence

WHAT THIS STYLE NEVER DOES:
→ Never fabricates facts not in evidence
→ Never takes a political or ideological stance
→ Never mocks or belittles anyone in the courtroom
→ Never overdramatizes real people's genuine pain
→ Never predicts outcomes that aren't shown

OUTPUT FORMAT:

[TIMESTAMP] | NARRATION LINE | (word count) | [BEAT]

BEAT TYPES:
[introduce] — presenting the case and parties
[setup] — explaining the legal background
[context] — giving emotional or relational background
[revelation] — a key fact or detail revealed in court
[confrontation] — direct emotional exchange
[argument] — legal reasoning or dispute
[human_moment] — when the human story breaks through
[reaction] — how the judge or others respond
[peak] — the single most dramatic moment
[pivot] — when the case takes an unexpected turn
[closing] — wrapping up the presented portion
[lesson] — the takeaway or moral

PHASE 5 — LESSON + CLOSING + CTA
════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LESSON (delivered right after the peak moment):
After the climactic moment of the case — deliver
one short sentence of wisdom. This is the MORAL
of the story.

LESSON FORMULAS:
"Sometimes the people closest to us see things
 we refuse to see in ourselves."

"Family and the law rarely mix well —
 because love and logic speak different languages."

"Being right in court doesn't always mean
 being right as a parent."

"The hardest cases are never about the law.
 They're always about what the law cannot fix."

"A parent's love and a child's needs —
 sometimes they don't line up the way they should."

"Every case has two sides. And sometimes
 both sides are hurting."

CLOSING LINE:
After the lesson — one final line that lands
the emotional weight and makes the viewer reflect.

CLOSING FORMULAS:
"For one [role], the legal battle was over.
 But the emotional one — was just beginning."

"The judge made a decision.
 But some questions don't have courtroom answers."

"In the end, this wasn't really about [legal issue].
 It was about something far more human."

"The courtroom fell silent.
 Because everyone in that room understood the weight of what just happened."

CUSTOM CTA:
→ Directly asks viewer their opinion on the case
→ Invites them to take a side or share their experience
→ Maximum 15 words
→ Ends with a question

CTA FORMULAS SPECIFIC TO COURTROOM CONTENT:
"Do you think the [role] was right? Or the [other role]? Tell me below."

"What would you have done in this situation?
 Drop your thoughts in the comments."

"Have you ever faced a situation like this?
 Share your story below."

"Was the [role] asking too much — or was the [other role]
 being unreasonable? I want to know."

"Who do you agree with — the [role] or the [other role]?
 Let me know."
